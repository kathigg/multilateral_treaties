A memorandum discussing more fully the dispute over sovereignty is enclosed.2 There is no way to resolve this issue by compromise, since each side believes that it has sovereignty. The only method by which it can now be resolved, in my judgment, is to have the question of sovereignty decided by an impartial tribunal, the International Court of Justice. High officials of the Honduran Government have given indications that Honduras would probably agree to this course. A statement regarding the relevance of the Connelly Amendment3 is enclosed.4 It is likely that the United States will win the case if it is submitted. However, even if this were not the situation, I believe it would still be highly desirable for the United States to manifest, particularly in its relations with a small country such as Honduras, its readiness in appropriate cases to accept the resolution of a dispute by an impartial tribunal in accordance with international law. Submission of the Swan Islands dispute to the International Court of Justice appears to offer the best available course of action for the United States in its relations with Honduras, and it has been the policy of the United States to support and foster the peaceful settlement of disputes by the International Court of Justice in appropriate cases. The Department of Defense and the Central Intelligence Agency have no objection to such a course of action. I request that you authorize me to seek agreement with the Government of Honduras for the submission of this dispute to the International Court of Justice.5 Christian A. Herter 2 Not printed.
