On February 24, 1960, at Quito, Ambassador Ravndal and Foreign 
Minister Tobar signed an Agreement Relating to a Cooperative Program 
in Ecuador for the Observation and Tracking of Satellites and Space 
Vehicles. It provided for continuing the operation and maintenance of 
the satellite tracking station at Paramo de Cotopaxi, known as the Quito 
Tracking Station Facility. For the text of the agreement, see 11 UST 179.
EC–13.	
Editorial Note
In despatch 381 from Quito, March 6, 1960, the Embassy reported 
that Ecuadoran Minister of Defense Gustavo Diez Delgado had 
accepted the invitation of Secretary of Defense Thomas S. Gates, Jr., 
to visit Washington and various United States military installations, 
March 7–17. (722.00(W)/3–460) In a news conference of March 26, 
Defense Minister Diez, who had returned to Quito on March 18, stated 
that the United States would supply Ecuador with $18 million in mil-
itary equipment. As reported in despatch 431 from Quito on April 1, 
he expressed complete satisfaction with his conversations with United 
States defense officials, stressing the economic advantage of United 
States military aid. (722.00(W)/4–160)
EC–14.	
Editorial Note
In a memorandum of conversation with Assistant Secretary of 
State Roy R. Rubottom, Jr., on March 14, 1960, Ambassador José R. 
Chiriboga Villagómez referred to a recent Ecuadoran request for a 
sugar quota of 35,000 tons in 1960. Assistant Secretary Rubottom 
replied that the problem was under study. (822.235/3–1460) In a note 
of July 21, 1960, to Ambassador Ravndal, Foreign Minister Tobar raised 
the Ecuadoran request to a 40,000 ton sugar quota in the fourth quarter 
of 1960. (822.235/7–2660) The Secretary of State informed Ambassador 
Chiriboga in a note dated August 5, 1960, that the United States could 
not consider Ecuador as a source of sugar in the reassignment of Cuba’s 
quota for 1960. (822.235/7–1160)
