El Salvador 617 We are convinced from pattern of coup, make-­up and early actions of government, Communist tones of decrees and announcements, and especially yesterday’s press conference by junta (EMBTEL 231)3 that military element no match for civilian leftists and that as matters now stand, we must regard coup as first step towards Fidelismo. For moment announcements seem calculated to lull us but clearly next stage will be struggle for power between Commies and more conservative element and military with odds favoring former. If remedial measures to be successful they will have to be taken within next few days while army still has control of arms. Reports reaching us indicate that while many viewed coup with approval at outset believing Osorio was the man, their ardor has cooled considerably after seeing make-­up of government. A counter coup effort, therefore, which our information indicates may be in making, may find more public support outside leftist elements than could have been hoped for earlier. View all these considerations and many still unanswered questions, believe strongly that our traditional policy of recognition should not be following in this instance. If our fears confirmed by subsequent action, major and practically only weapon available to U.S., since intervention renounced under international agreement, is recognition and/ or nonrecognition. Believe from talks with colleagues our action in this respect will be closely watched and followed by number of other countries. I urge with all persuasiveness at my command the Department go slow with recognition. Kalijarvi
