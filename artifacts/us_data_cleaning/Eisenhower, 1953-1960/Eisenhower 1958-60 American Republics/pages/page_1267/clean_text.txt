Uruguay 1179 SUMMARY: Although the conversation offered a very good opportunity for the Secretary to explain fully our positions vis-à-­vis Cuba, the Uruguayan Foreign Minister did not respond with any assurances that Uruguay understood it was a hemisphere problem nor did he give any indication of any official Uruguayan position on the Cuban case. He did make the point that, in the minds of the Uruguayans, the Cuban and Dominican problems are linked, and should be considered together. This, of course, was a reflection of the very strong anti-Dominican attitude in Uruguay. The Minister soon wondered off the Cuban question and started talking in generalities about the world of situation. Whether this was deliberate or merely represented his lack of coordinated thinking on the problem is conjectural. UR–16. Despatch from the Ambassador in Uruguay (Woodward) to the Department of State1  Montevideo, August 26, 1960. No. 155. SUBJECT Current Political and Economic Situation in Uruguay Summary Relations between the Uruguayan Government and the United States Government are at present very cooperative. This is important to the United States because Uruguay still possesses special international prestige. And Uruguayan governmental cooperation is well supported by Uruguayan public opinion. There is increasing awareness in Uruguay of the danger of international communism; Uruguayans consider Uruguay impregnable to communism; but communist and Cuban propaganda is persistent, and communist influence in Uruguay is strong among a minority of the labor movement and a minority of the teachers.
