Anticipated Subjects That President-­elect Echandi May Discuss:
(1) The Costa Rican Financial Situation
Costa Rica’s international reserves have declined from $22 million 
at the end of 1955 to $13.3 million at the end of 1957. This drop has been 
occasioned by a substantial deficit in the 1956 balance of payments aris-
ing from a decrease in agricultural production for both export and the 
domestic market, a rise in monetary income chiefly from expansion in 
domestic credit and a continued high rate of imports. Although 1957 
foreign exchange receipts were high, imports and other payments con-
tinued their upward trend, and 1957 ended with no appreciable increase 
in the country’s reserves. In a recent report made by the International 
Monetary Fund, it was concluded that Costa Rica is not prepared to 
withstand major downward fluctuations in the international prices 
of its main export commodities (coffee and bananas) nor to cope with 
adverse developments in export crop production with the present level 
of exchange reserves.
