200 Foreign Relations, 1958–1960, Volume III, Supplement Comment When occasion permits you might make the following additional comment: There are many types of war or crises situations requiring many types of weapons to meet those situations. If we narrow the spec­ trum of our weapons system to nuclear weapons or if we are only prepared to use large-­scale military measures designed to “defeat” the enemy, we may well find ourselves frozen into inaction in situa­ tions where we would properly hesitate to apply the required atomic weapon (as in infiltrations, evacuations, guerilla fighting, etc.). On the other hand, if we were to plunge into these situations from the outset with nuclear weapons or with excessive retaliatory measures, we not only would risk a major nuclear war but would forego all possibili­ ties of settlement involved in diplomacy conducted at the outset of a conflict before it spreads into the nuclear field or before it spreads geographically.
