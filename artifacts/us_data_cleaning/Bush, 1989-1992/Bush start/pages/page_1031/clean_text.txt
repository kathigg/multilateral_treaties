If the SS–N–18 is treated as a downloaded missile, the Soviet approach amounts to the right to download up to 5 RVs from each of three types of missiles, within an aggregate limit of about 2000 RVs. Possible Downloading Options There are three variables that could be used to define downloading options: (1) the number of types of missiles that could be downloaded; (2) the number of RVs that could be downloaded from each missile; and, (3) the aggregate number of RVs that could be downloaded. Each of the following options assumes that the SS–N–18 would be captured as a downloaded missile, that base-­by-­base downloading would be allowed, that downloading of heavy ICBMs would be banned, and that there would be one extra RV inspection per year per base where downloaded missiles are deployed. A. Grandfather SS–N–18 and MM III RV attribution (current US position). – Download 4 RVs per missile for SS–N–18. – Download 2 RV per missile for MM III. – No aggregate limit. Aggregate downloading: US—600–1000 RVs

Soviet—896 RVs B. Download an aggregate of 1000 RVs for each side on existing types of missiles. – Either ICBMs or SLBMs. – No limit on the number of different types of missiles. – As types of downloaded missiles are retired, additional existing types may be downloaded. Aggregate downloading: US—1000 RVs

Soviet—1000 RVs C. Grandfather the SS–N–18 and MM III attribution separate from downloading. Download up to two RVs per missile on one additional type of existing missile with an aggregate limit of 500 RVs. —Either ICBMs or SLBMs. Aggregate downloading: US—1080–1480 RVs

Soviet—1136 RVs D. Download up to two RVs per missile on three existing types of missiles. – Except the SS–N–18 could be downloaded by four RVs per missile. – Either ICBMs or SLBMs – No aggregate limit. Aggregate downloading: US—1464–1864 RVs

Soviet—1320 RVs
