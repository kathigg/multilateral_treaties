Third, Nazarkin says Shevardnadze wants to close out noncircum-
vention. While the Soviets prefer a U.S. or U.S./U.K. statement, they 
appear willing to make a unilateral statement saying that if we were to 
expand cooperation with the British beyond SLBMs, the Soviets would 
consider such an expansion grounds for withdrawing from START. 
As part of settling the non-­circumvention issue, Nazarkin believes the 
related issues of third country basing an inspections [less than 1 line not 
declassified] could also be resolved.
Fourth, Nazarkin says that if we make progress on other issues, 
such as non-­circumvention, the Soviets will be willing to solve the issue 
of conventional ALCMs with multiple warheads on the basis of a letter from 
you making clear that we would not use development of such weapons 
as a means to circumvent the ban on nuclear ALCMs with multiple 
warheads. The text of a proposed letter, which has been cleared by the 
arms control group, is attached.4
In sum, I think we are well-­positioned to get a lot done this week. 
That said, I am very worried about the space deployment and test-
ing problem. If we end up asking the Soviets to agree—­in explicit 
language—­that we can test weapons in orbit, it would be tantamount to 
“reverse linkage”: Making their acceptance of the broad interpretation 
of the ABM Treaty a precondition to our agreeing to START.
