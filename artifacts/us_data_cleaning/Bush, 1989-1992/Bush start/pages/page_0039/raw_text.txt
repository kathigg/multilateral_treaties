More than a few Europeans wanted me to know how much reas-
surance they took from your governmental experience and preparation 
for the Presidency. In Europe, it is difficult for a newcomer to break 
into national politics—­one gets there only by long training. A French 
Socialist said he’d vote for you.
One theme ran through comments at NATO and the London 
conference—­the Discriminant Deterrence study that Fred Ikle, Albert 
Wohlstetter, Henry Kissinger, Zbig and others did should not be 
the basis for future national security planning. Few Americans or 
Europeans had much good to say about it. One went so far as to say 
it adopted the Soviet view: one continuum of weapons—­a merger of 
nuclear and conventional forces where nuclear forces are only more 
powerful artillery pieces. Others say it pushes changes in NATO’s basic 
strategy of defense changing it to a new and unwanted offensive strat-
egy of attacks deep into Eastern Europe. It is seen as nullifying flexi-
ble response, and seriously de-­linking us from Europe by confining a 
nuclear war to Europe. It is seen as a change being forced on NATO by 
the US without consultation and study—­the Europeans I spoke with 
have nothing against thinking about, or rethinking the strategy—­they 
want to be part of the study, not hear about it after “you Americans” 
decide to “publish a pamphlet.”
