811.2300/86: Telegram
[Document 187]
The Ambassador in Japan (Woods) to the Secretary of State
TOKYO, March 22, 1924—2 p.m.
[Received March 23—12:15 p.m.]
54. Department’s 37, March 8, 6 p.m. Vice Minister of Foreign Affairs
notified me informally this morning that he had finally succeeded in
securing the consent of the Japanese military and naval authorities to allow
one American destroyer to proceed to Bettobu and another destroyer to
Kashiwabara as desired by our War Department under following conditions:
“1. This expedition to be solely for the purpose of
taking essential supplies.
2. The destroyers are to stop at Yokohama to take on
board one Japanese Army and one Japanese naval
officer who are to remain on board during the trip to the
Kurile Islands.”
I desire to emphasize the fact that this permission has been secured solely
through the personal intervention of the Vice Minister of Foreign Affairs
and that throughout these negotiations the Embassy had had to contend with
the vigorous opposition of the Japanese military and naval authorities who
had been opposed in principle to the flight and categorically opposed to
allowing any foreign warship to enter the ports of the Kurile Islands which
they say have always been regarded as closed territory, as set forth in my
telegram number 200, December 21, 8 p.m.25 The Japanese War Office had
definitely declared to the military attaché at this Embassy that the only
flight the War Office would consent to would be a nonstop flight from the
Aleutian Islands to Aomori. In view of this attitude of the Army and Navy
Departments it was found necessary to appeal to Vice Minister for Foreign
Affairs who through his personal influence has been able finally to have the
wishes of our War Department carried out.
